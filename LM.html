Linear Models (LM)

Let $y_1, y_2, ..., y_n$ be the responses from $n$ subjects, each response follows independently a normal distribution: \[ y_i \sim N(\mu_i, \sigma^2), i=1, 2, \cdots, n \]

A linear model is to relate the mean $\mu_i$ with some characterics of each subject, $x_{i1}, x_{i2}, \cdots, x_{ik}$, called predictors, covariates, independent variables, etc. These covariates are considered to have potential effects on the mean of the response: /[ \mu_i=\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1}+ \beta_2 x_{i2} + \cdots + \beta_k x_{ik}\] or /[ \y_i=\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1}+ \beta_2 x_{i2} + \cdots + \beta_k x_{ik} + \epsilon_i, \epsilon_i \sim N(0, \sigma^2) \] where $\epsilon_i$ is the random variation in y that is not explained by the covariates in the model. The random variation may be due to other variables that affect y but are not known or not observed, or simply measurement errors. The parameters $\beta_0, \beta_1, ..., \beta_k $ are called regression coefficients. \[ y=X \beta + \epsilon, \epsilon \sim N(0, \sigma^2I_n) \]
